Playing out football matches through Twitter

Introduction       Visualisation

This page shows a Twitter visualisation for the final Tottenham Hotspur match of 2012/2013 season, versus Sunderland AFC. The hashtag '#COYS' (standing for 'Come On You Spurs') was tracked for the duration of the match, and a few hours either side. Tottenham won the match 1-0, with Gareth Bale scoring in the 89th minute, which you can clearly see in the data. Some of the tweets also refer to Arsenal's simultaneous match, whose outcome would affect Tottenham's final league standing for the season. The tweets have been analysed for a variety of factors, including sentiment and passion. The sentiment is based on a Bag of Words model, and doesn't take into account the syntax of a sentence (e.g. negatives, double negatives). Passion is based on usage of the exclamation mark(!)

Looking first at the volume of tweets, we see a steady rise towards the start of the match, and then a decrease through play, as people (thankfully) become more engrossed in the match than their phones. At the end of the match though there is a surge, as people reflect on what has just happened (Tottenham were devastatingly pipped to the post of Champion's League qualification by arch-rivals Arsenal, who beat Newcastle in their simultaneous fixture).

Turning to the passion metric, we see a huge spike right at the end of the match. This was due to the (incredible) goal scored by Gareth Bale in the 89th minute. The two other spikes in passion - one in the first and one in the second half - were both due to the referee denying Tottenham some (blatant) penalties.

Finally, looking at the positive/negative sentiment through the match, we see that the tweets during the match were generally very low on positive sentiment, due to a tense game. There were spikes in negative sentiment though at the penalty calls, generally through swearing. At the final whistle the tweets become a lot more emotional, with a surge in both positive and negative sentiment.


By David Abelman 2013.
